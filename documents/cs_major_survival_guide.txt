The Unofficial Brookfield CS Major Survival Guide
(maintained by upperclassmen — not an official department document)

INTRODUCTION
This guide collects the advice we wish someone had given us as freshmen. The official course
catalog tells you the prerequisites and credit hours. It does not tell you which professors
will change how you think, which classes will eat your entire semester, or the order to take
things in so you don't set yourself up to fail. That's what this guide is for.

RECOMMENDED COURSE SEQUENCE
Year 1: Start with CS 101 (Intro to Programming) with Okafor if you have little or no coding
experience. It's gentle, beginner-friendly, and builds a real foundation. Take your math
sequence in parallel — calculus and especially LINEAR ALGEBRA. You will need linear algebra
later for machine learning and graphics, and putting it off is the single most common planning
mistake CS majors here make.

Year 2: CS 201 (Data Structures) with Marsh is the gateway to the major. Be warned: the jump
from Okafor's gentle 101 to Marsh's rigorous 201 is the steepest difficulty increase you'll
face. Marsh's exams come entirely from her lecture slides, so attendance is effectively
mandatory, and the weekly problem sets run 8-10 hours. Midterms are curved; the final is not.

Year 3: This is your heaviest year. CS 310 (Algorithms) with Patel demands strong proof skills
and a lot of independent reading from CLRS — his exams are far harder than the homework. CS 340
(Operating Systems) with Lin is the most time-intensive class in the major: 15+ hours a week of
C programming projects done in groups of three. The hard rule from everyone who survived it:
NEVER take Patel and Lin in the same semester. Pair each with lighter electives.

Year 4: Specialize. CS 420 (Machine Learning) with Reyes is a favorite, but only attempt it
after you've completed linear algebra and a statistics course — there is no curve, and the
first two weeks are pure matrix math. CS 330 (Databases) with Becker is the classic GPA cushion:
open-note exams, light workload, easy A, though you won't go deep on the material.

WORKLOAD BALANCING
The golden rule: never stack two project-heavy or two exam-brutal courses in the same term.
A common safe pattern is to pair one killer (Lin or Patel) with Becker's databases to keep your
total workload survivable. Plan your semesters around the hard classes, not the easy ones.

OFFICE HOURS
Use them, and use them EARLY in the semester. Marsh's and Reyes's office hours get packed in
the week before exams. The students who do best are the ones who show up in week two with
questions, not week ten in a panic.
